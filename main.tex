\title{Symplectic Resolution of Semiclassical Singularities}

\begin{abstract}
We identify Planck’s quantum of action not as a static cell in phase space, but as a dynamic symplectic geometry. This conjugate structure resolves semiclassical singularities, replacing divergent caustics with finite quantum densities.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}

Semiclassical mechanics maps classical orbits to oscillating quantum states. Yet it fails at physical boundaries, predicting infinite probability caustics at classical turning points. 

This failure stems from the assumption of infinite kinematic action. To achieve spatial localization at a turning point, the conjugate momentum must diverge to infinity. This geometric extreme demands an infinite amount of action.

Physical systems do not possess infinite action. They are governed by the finite classical action $A$ and the invariant quantum of action $h$. Here, we treat $h$ not as a rigid phase-space cell, but as a dynamic symplectic geometry. By kinematically bounding the spatial resolution to the finite action of the system, we forbid this geometric collapse, resolving the infinite caustics into finite probability amplitudes.

\section{The Symplectic Quantum of Action}

De Gosson \cite{degosson2009} translates the statistical Heisenberg limit, $\sigma_q \sigma_p \ge \hbar/2$, into classical semi-axes $\Delta q = \sqrt{2}\sigma_q$ and $\Delta p = \sqrt{2}\sigma_p$. This maps the statistical limit to the classical action area:
$$A = \pi \Delta q \Delta p \ge \frac{h}{2}.$$
With this equivalence established, we discard the statistical interpretation; hereafter, $\Delta q$ and $\Delta p$ serve as classical kinematic boundaries that define the geometric area $h/2$.

The interior of this Hamiltonian boundary is structured by Zurek's reciprocal phase space scales \cite{zurek2001}. Rooted in quantum metrology \cite{caves1981, meekhof1996}, spatial resolution is governed by the conjugate momentum boundary, $\delta q = \hbar / \Delta p$, and vice versa, $\delta p = \hbar / \Delta q$. 

Expressed as phase space areas, these metrological bounds form a pair of centered, orthogonal ellipses:
$$a_q = \pi \delta q \Delta p = \frac{h}{2} \quad \text{and} \quad a_p = \pi \Delta q \delta p = \frac{h}{2}.$$
These constitute a conjugate pair of de Gosson's irreducible quantum blobs.

We therefore identify Planck's constant as the sum of this conjugate pair:
$$h = a_q + a_p.$$
Geometrically, the quantum of action is a dynamic, symplectic structure (see Fig.~\ref{fig:quantum_of_action}).

In the normalized harmonic ground state, the blobs degenerate into a perfect circle where the classical action meets the quantum floor, $A = a_q = a_p = h/2$ (see Fig.~\ref{fig:qho_grid}, left column, top row). As $A$ increases, this symplectic exchange compresses the quantum blobs under the topological constraint of Gromov's non-squeezing theorem \cite{gromov1985}, shrinking $a_q$ and $a_p$, enforcing the correspondence principle.

While each blob upholds the invariant quantum floor ($h/2$), the squeezing via classical action $A$ collapses the orthogonal blobs $a_q$ and $a_p$ into fine crosshairs (see Fig.~\ref{fig:qho_grid}, left column, bottom row).

\section{Semiclassical Singularities}

While the semiclassical WKB approximation \cite{langer1937} translates classical trajectories into quantum amplitudes, its spatial probability density fails at the classical boundaries. The 1D projection relies on the classical momentum $p(q)$, modulated by a geometric phase:
\begin{equation}
P_{\text{WKB}}(q) \propto \frac{1}{p(q)} \cos^2\left(\frac{A_{\text{chord}}(q)}{\hbar} - \frac{\pi}{4}\right).
\end{equation}

At the turning points where classical momentum vanishes ($p \to 0$), the envelope exhibits a caustic divergence:
\begin{equation}
\lim_{q \to q_t} P_{\text{WKB}}(q) = \infty.
\end{equation}

Confined by these boundaries, the WKB approximation traps the projected spatial density between infinite caustics. This behavior represents a breakdown of the 1D semiclassical projection. Bridging these kinematic boundaries with finite observables requires a mechanism that accounts for finite action.

\section{Symplectic Resolution}

To resolve these singularities, we deploy the symplectic quantum of action, $h = a_q + a_p$, as a symplectic kernel $K_h$. We map the divergent 1D projection $P_{\text{WKB}}(q)$ to a symplectic density $P_h(q)$ by convolving it with the position support $\delta q$ of the quantum blob $a_q$.

Because the spatial support $\delta q$ is bounded by the reciprocal momentum, $\delta q = \hbar / \Delta p$, we define the symplectic kernel $K_h$ as the normalized Gaussian profile of this geometric area. Substituting for $\delta q$ reveals the classical momentum, $\Delta p$, regulating the position:
$$K_h(q) = \frac{2\sqrt{\pi} \Delta p}{h} \exp\left( - \left( \frac{2\pi \Delta p \, q}{h} \right)^2 \right).$$

The symplectic density $P_h(q)$ is obtained through the convolution of the semiclassical density $P_{\text{WKB}}(q)$ with this symplectic kernel $K_h$:
$$P_h(q) = (P_{\text{WKB}} * K_h)(q) = \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} P_{\text{WKB}}(q') K_h(q - q') \, dq'.$$

Whereas the Husimi distribution \cite{husimi1940} applies a rigid $\hbar/2$ coarse-graining, the symplectic kernel $K_h$ is squeezed by the macroscopic boundaries $\Delta q$ and $\Delta p$, binding the spatial resolution $\delta q = \hbar/\Delta p$ and conserving the quantum of action. This spatial blur extends past classical turning points, yielding quantum tunneling. As macroscopic momentum grows, the resolution tightens. The evanescent tails shrink, geometrically enforcing the correspondence principle.

Although the semiclassical density diverges at the turning points, the singularity scales as $1/\sqrt{q}$, meaning the localized probability mass remains finite. Sustaining an infinite spatial density requires the conjugate momentum boundary to diverge to infinity---a geometric extreme that demands infinite action.

Because the available action is limited by the symplectic quantum of action $h$, this infinite collapse is forbidden. Integrating the semiclassical singularities against the symplectic kernel $K_h$ distributes the finite probability mass across the $\delta q$ width of the quantum blob. The presence of $h$ prevents the kinematic collapse, transforming the non-physical caustics into finite, observable probability amplitudes.

\section{The Harmonic Oscillator}

We demonstrate the symplectic resolution of semiclassical singularities via the harmonic oscillator (see Fig.~\ref{fig:qho_grid}). Following Berry's semiclassical formulation \cite{berry1977}, the 2D Wigner phase-space distribution \cite{wigner1932} oscillates according to the geometric area $A_{\text{chord}}(q,p)$ bounded between the classical trajectory and a chord bisected by $(q,p)$:
$$W(q,p) \propto \cos\left( \frac{A_{\text{chord}}(q,p)}{\hbar} - \frac{\pi}{4} \right).$$

Standard projections of this 2D distribution onto the 1D spatial axis treat the classical boundary as an infinitely sharp curve, inevitably spawning unphysical geometric caustics. 

Rather than patching these turning points with Airy functions \cite{langer1937}, we execute our 2D symplectic projection. Integrating the raw geometric density against the symplectic kernel $K_h$ enforces the irreducible phase-space area $a_q = h/2$. This bounded spatial resolution physically truncates the caustic divergence, seamlessly transforming the classical boundaries into the finite probability amplitudes characteristic of discrete quantum states.

\section{Discussion}

Semiclassical caustics are not physical. They are artifacts of assuming infinite kinematic action. 

When action is finite, these singularities vanish. Classical action $A=\pi \Delta q \Delta p$ squeezes the conjugate coordinate resolutions $\delta q = \hbar / \Delta p$ and $\delta p = \hbar / \Delta q$, but the invariant quantum of action, $h$, forbids a collapse to zero. 

The divergent caustics represent a mathematical extrapolation to infinity. By instead treating macroscopic action as a finite, physical resource that scales the quantum-to-classical transition, these unphysical boundary collapses are forbidden, naturally resolving into finite quantum densities.
