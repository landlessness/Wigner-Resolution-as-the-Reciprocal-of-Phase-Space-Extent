\section{The Symplectic Quantum of Action}

De Gosson \cite{degosson2009} geometrically characterizes the statistical Heisenberg limit, $\sigma_q \sigma_p \ge \hbar/2$, via classical semi-axes $\Delta q = \sqrt{2}\sigma_q$ and $\Delta p = \sqrt{2}\sigma_p$, establishing the macroscopic action area:
$$A = \pi \Delta q \Delta p \ge \frac{h}{2}.$$

The interior of this Hamiltonian boundary is structured by Zurek's reciprocal phase space scales \cite{zurek2001}. Rooted in quantum metrology \cite{caves1981}, spatial resolution is funded by the conjugate momentum spread, $\delta q = \hbar / \Delta p$, and vice versa, $\delta p = \hbar / \Delta q$. 

Expressed as phase space areas, these metrological bounds form a pair of centered, orthogonal ellipses:
$$ a_q = \pi \delta q \Delta p = \frac{h}{2} \quad \text{and} \quad a_p = \pi \Delta q \delta p = \frac{h}{2}. $$ 
These are precisely a conjugate pair of de Gosson's minimum-uncertainty quantum blobs.

We therefore identify the quantum of action (see Fig.~\ref{fig:quantum_of_action}) as the sum of this conjugate pair:
$$h = a_q + a_p.$$
This reframes Planck's constant from an indivisible cell of phase space into a dynamic, symplectic form.

The intersection of these orthogonal blobs encloses an ellipse of area $ a = \pi \delta q \delta p $, where $ a \le h/2 \le A $. In the ground state, this hierarchy degenerates into a perfect circle where $A = a_q = a_p = a = h/2$ (see Fig.~\ref{fig:qho_grid}, left column, top row). As $A$ increases, this symplectic exchange compresses the quantum blobs under the topological constraint of Gromov's non-squeezing theorem \cite{gromov1985}, shrinking $a$ and geometrically enforcing the correspondence principle:
$$a = \frac{(\pi \hbar)^2}{A}.$$

While each blob upholds the invariant quantum floor ($h/2$), the squeezing via classical action $A$ collapses the orthogonal blobs $a_q$ and $a_p$ into fine crosshairs and the interior phase-space support $a$ into a point (see Fig.~\ref{fig:qho_grid}, left column, bottom row), forbidding macroscopic uncertainty.

\section{Semiclassical Singularities}

While the WKB approximation successfully captures the phase of highly excited states, its spatial probability density fails fundamentally at the classical turning points. The classical probability density $P_{\text{cl}}(q)$ is inversely proportional to the classical momentum $p(q)$:
$$P_{\text{cl}}(q) = \frac{N}{p(q)} = \frac{N}{\sqrt{2m(E - V(q))}}$$

At the turning points where $E = V(q)$, the momentum vanishes ($p \to 0$). Consequently, the classical density exhibits a non-physical caustic divergence:
$$P_{\text{cl}}(q) \to \infty$$
This singularity represents a breakdown of the semiclassical model, requiring a mechanism to bridge the classical boundaries with finite quantum observables.

\section{Symplectic Resolution}

To resolve these singularities, we deploy the symplectic quantum of action, $h = a_q + a_p$, as a Symplectic Kernel. Rather than relying on standard wave equations, we map the classical density to a physically realizable quantum distribution by convolving the classical skeleton with the spatial footprint of the squeezed state. 

Recalling that the spatial resolution is bounded by the reciprocal momentum, $\delta q = h / (2\pi \Delta p)$, we define the Symplectic Kernel as the normalized Gaussian profile of the quantum blob $a_q$. Substituting for $\delta q$, we reveal Planck's quantum of action, $h$, explicitly regulating the distribution:
$$K(q) = \frac{2\sqrt{\pi} \Delta p}{h} \exp\left( - \left( \frac{2\pi \Delta p \, q}{h} \right)^2 \right)$$

The true physical density, $P_{\text{qm}}(q)$, is obtained through the convolution of the divergent classical density with this Symplectic Kernel:
$$P_{\text{qm}}(q) = (P_{\text{cl}} * K)(q) = \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} P_{\text{cl}}(q') K(q - q') \, dq'$$

Because the classical singularity goes as $1/\sqrt{q}$, it is mathematically integrable. Sweeping the Symplectic Kernel across the phase space strictly bounds the divergence; the presence of $h$ in the denominator of the kernel structurally prevents classical collapse, naturally resolving the infinite caustics into a smooth, finite, $\mathcal{C}^\infty$ distribution.
