\title{Wigner Resolution as the Reciprocal of Phase-Space Extent}

\begin{abstract}
The Heisenberg principle bounds a quantum state's outer extent in phase space. A complementary inner scale, set by the same quantum of action, is reached independently by two routes: de Gosson's symplectic polar duality of the covariance ellipsoid, and Zurek's interference-driven sub-Planck chessboard. Both arrive at $\delta q = \hbar/\Delta p$ and $\delta p = \hbar/\Delta q$ from the state's covariance, with different physical readings. Convolution of the Wigner function with the squeezed-coherent kernels at these inner scales yields non-negative densities $P_{\delta q}$ and $P_{\delta p}$ whose marginals reproduce $|\psi(q)|^2$ and $|\hat\psi(p)|^2$ across eight non-Gaussian states to within small residuals set by $\delta q = \hbar/\Delta p$ and $\delta p = \hbar/\Delta q$ respectively. The standard inverse-Radon reconstruction of $W$ from measured marginals accepts negativity as fundamental; the same marginals also support non-negative reconstructions at the state's intrinsic resolutions.
\end{abstract}

\label{fig:heisenberg_cells}

The Wigner function \cite{wigner1932} is the standard joint phase-space representation of a quantum state. Non-Gaussian states yield Wigner functions that take negative values \cite{hudson1974}, a feature that has come to be read as a quantum signature in tomography \cite{leonhardt1997} and as a resource for quantum advantage \cite{mari2012}. A parallel literature treats non-negative phase-space densities as the operative object: truncated Wigner approximations and Husimi distributions for semiclassical and open-system dynamics, lab homodyne reconstructions where measurement noise is large compared to negativity, and resource-theoretic accounting that asks at what resolution a state's negativity persists \cite{veitch2014}. These methods agree on the value of a non-negative density but disagree on the kernel: each fixes the resolution by an external choice---harmonic vacuum, ordering convention, or sampling width---rather than by the state itself. We show that the state's own covariance fixes a unique non-negative resolution, set by the reciprocal of the state's phase-space extent.

% ===============================================================================
%  SLICE 1, PIECE B --- de Gosson
% ===============================================================================

A quantum state's covariance defines the extent of its extended cell\footnote{Heisenberg cells are conventionally drawn as rectangles for visual simplicity; the correct symplectic-geometric object is an ellipsoid \cite{degosson2013qblobs, degossoncharlyne2022}. Throughout this Letter, ``cell'' denotes the ellipsoid.}, the phase-space ellipsoid with semi-axes $\Delta q = \sqrt{2}\sigma_q$, $\Delta p = \sqrt{2}\sigma_p$ and area
\begin{equation}
A = \pi\,\Delta q\,\Delta p \ge h/2,
\label{eq:extended_cell}
\end{equation}
bounded below by Heisenberg \cite{robertson1929,schrodinger1930}. De Gosson identifies a complementary ellipsoid inscribed in the extended cell \cite{degosson2013qblobs, degossoncharlyne2022}: the squeezed cell has area exactly $h/2$,
\begin{equation}
a = \pi\,\delta q\,\delta p = h/2,
\label{eq:squeezed_cell}
\end{equation}
with semi-axes $\delta q$ and $\delta p$ obeying the polar-dual identity and $\delta q \le \Delta q$ and $\delta p \le \Delta p$. Theorem~13 of \cite{degossoncharlyne2022} establishes that a centered phase-space ellipsoid is a squeezed cell if and only if the polar dual of its position projection equals its momentum intersection. Gromov's non-squeezing theorem \cite{gromov1985} forbids any canonical compression below $h/2$.

% ===============================================================================
%  SLICE 1, PIECE C --- tomography
% ===============================================================================

% ===============================================================================
%  SLICE 1, PIECE D --- Zurek
% ===============================================================================

Zurek expands a generic state as a superposition of coherent states spread over the extended cell and identifies the smallest interference scales of the Wigner function \cite{zurek2001}. With the state's extended cell $A$ providing the spatial scales $\Delta q$ and $\Delta p$, the smallest interference periods are
\begin{equation}
\delta q = \hbar/\Delta p, \qquad \delta p = \hbar/\Delta q.
\label{eq:zurek}
\end{equation}
These are de Gosson's polar-dual semi-axes \eqref{eq:squeezed_cell} at the principal-axis orientations. Zurek shows that they control the state's sensitivity to phase-space displacements, and hence its decoherence rate.

Two independent derivations---symplectic polar duality and interference geometry---reach the same scales. This convergence is the starting point of this Letter.

% ======================================================================
% SLICE 2 --- THE DENSITY
% ======================================================================

The Wigner--Weyl distribution \cite{wigner1932}
\begin{equation}
W(q,p) = \frac{1}{\pi\hbar}\int \psi^*(q+y)\,\psi(q-y)\,e^{2ipy/\hbar}\, dy
\label{eq:wigner}
\end{equation}
takes negative values for any non-Gaussian state \cite{hudson1974}. By \eqref{eq:reciprocal} the pointwise limit $\delta q \to 0$ requires $\Delta p \to \infty$ and unbounded covariance area: pointwise sampling of $W$ asks a finer question than the uncertainty principle permits, and the negativity is the formalism's report.

The squeezed-coherent kernel matched to the state's covariance,
\begin{equation}
G_{\delta q}(q,p) = \frac{1}{\pi}\exp\!\left(-\frac{q^2}{\delta q^2} - \frac{p^2}{\Delta p^2}\right),
\label{eq:Gdq}
\end{equation}
is the Wigner transform of the squeezed coherent state at the state's covariance \cite{degossoncharlyne2022}, of area exactly $h/2$. The convolution
\begin{equation}
P_{\delta q}(q,p) = (W * G_{\delta q})(q,p)
\label{eq:Pdq}
\end{equation}
is non-negative everywhere, since convolution with any quantum-cell Wigner function yields a non-negative distribution \cite{degosson2005bullsci,degosson2013qblobs}. The polar-dual partner $P_{\delta p} = W * G_{\delta p}$ resolves momentum at $\delta p$ symmetrically, with
\begin{equation}
G_{\delta p}(q,p) = \frac{1}{\pi}\exp\!\left(-\frac{q^2}{\Delta q^2} - \frac{p^2}{\delta p^2}\right).
\label{eq:Gdp}
\end{equation}
The kernel is fixed by the state's covariance; no free parameter.

Figures~\ref{fig:wigner} and~\ref{fig:cats} show $P_{\delta q}$ for eight states with kernel set from each state's covariance, $\Delta q = \sqrt{2\langle (q-\langle q\rangle)^2\rangle}$, $\Delta p = \sqrt{2\langle p^2\rangle}$. The construction is non-negative across squeezed vacuum, harmonic eigenstates, Morse eigenstates with strong anharmonicity, double-well eigenstates with central-barrier interference, and four cat configurations.

Figure~\ref{fig:marginals} provides the quantitative spine. The marginal $\int P_{\delta q}(q,p)\,dp$ equals $|\psi(q)|^2$ convolved with a one-dimensional Gaussian of width $\delta q$ and is therefore the smoothed-at-$\delta q$ true position distribution by construction. Across the eight states, the integrated $L^1$ residual remains small: the smoothing is at the state's own scale, not at an externally imposed scale, and the deformation is the minimum the uncertainty principle permits. The same holds for $P_{\delta p}$ and $|\hat\psi(p)|^2$.

% ======================================================================
% SLICE 4 --- THE QUESTION
% ======================================================================

The standard tomographic pipeline measures marginals at many phase-space angles and reconstructs $W$ via inverse Radon transform, accepting negativity as fundamental \cite{leonhardt1997}. The measured marginals, however, also support a non-negative reconstruction: $P_{\delta q}$ and its rotated polar-dual partners reproduce the same marginals (Fig.~\ref{fig:marginals}) at the state's intrinsic resolution. Two reconstructions from the same data, one negative and one non-negative, differing in the resolution at which the Wigner function is sampled.

The construction is geometric; its empirical content is that any reconstructed $W$ admits the polar-dual smoothing without loss of marginal information. Whether and when a resolution-respecting non-negative reconstruction provides an alternative to the standard pipeline---in regimes where Wigner negativity complicates reconstruction or simulation, including finite-sample tomography \cite{leonhardt1997}, particle-based phase-space evolution, and the resource-theoretic accounting of negativity volume---is left to future work.

Convolution at scales other than $\delta q$ describes external effects rather than the state. Husimi's $Q$-function \cite{husimi1940} convolves $W$ with the harmonic-oscillator vacuum, recovering heterodyne detection statistics and the injected vacuum of a beam splitter's empty port \cite{leonhardt1997}; this coincides with the polar-dual kernel only at the harmonic ground state. The Cahill--Glauber $s$-parametrized family \cite{cahillglauber1969} is an operator-ordering interpolation \cite{wodkiewicz1984}. Dragoman's filtering \cite{dragoman2005} convolves with the measurement device. In each case the kernel is set by something external---vacuum, ordering convention, detector. $P_{\delta q}$ is the unique parameter-free smoothing whose kernel is set by the state's own covariance via Eqs.~\eqref{eq:reciprocal}.
