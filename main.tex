\title{Resolving Wigner Negativity and Semiclassical Caustics via Classical Action}

\begin{abstract}
Wigner negativity and semiclassical caustics both follow from assuming unbounded classical action. Enforcing the finite action of a physical system via symplectic phase-space cells resolves both simultaneously. The resulting phase-space densities are provably non-negative and free of turning-point divergences.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}

Phase-space representations of quantum mechanics bridge classical trajectories and quantum amplitudes, but the mapping introduces two persistent pathologies. The Wigner--Weyl distribution exhibits negative values wherever quantum interference is strong \cite{wigner1932}, while the WKB probability density diverges at classical turning points where the local momentum vanishes \cite{berry1977,langer1937}. These are typically treated as separate inconveniences of separate formalisms.

They are not. Both pathologies are kinematic consequences of the same assumption: that classical action is unbounded. Wigner negativity arises from interference on scales below $\hbar/\Delta p$; the WKB caustic arises from demanding $\delta q \to 0$ at the turning point, which forces $\Delta p \to \infty$ and hence infinite classical action. No physical system satisfies this condition.

The standard remedy applies a fixed Gaussian kernel to yield the Husimi Q-function \cite{husimi1940}, which suppresses negativity and softens the caustics. But the Husimi kernel is set once by the ground state and carries no information about the system's classical action --- a kernel decoupled from $A$ cannot resolve structure governed by $A$, and introduces artifactual tails broader than true quantum tunneling.

Here we enforce what the physics already demands: a finite classical action $A$ constrains the phase-space resolution to Zurek's reciprocal scales $\delta q = \hbar/\Delta p$ and $\delta p = \hbar/\Delta q$ \cite{zurek2001}. Drawing on de~Gosson's symplectic geometry \cite{degosson2009,degosson2012fermi} and Gromov's non-squeezing theorem \cite{gromov1985}, we construct squeezed Gaussian kernels governed entirely by $A$. These kernels saturate the Heisenberg limit, provably eliminate Wigner negativity by Hudson's theorem \cite{hudson1974}, and bound the momentum support at turning points, replacing WKB divergences with finite-amplitude densities. Finite classical action resolves Wigner negativity and semiclassical caustics.

\section{Wigner Negativity and Semiclassical Caustics}

The Wigner--Weyl distribution furnishes an exact phase-space representation of a quantum state $\psi$,
\begin{equation}
W(q,p) = \frac{1}{\pi\hbar}\int_{-\infty}^{\infty} \psi^*(q+x)\psi(q-x)\, e^{2ipx/\hbar}\, dx,
\end{equation}
but allows negative values wherever quantum interference is strong \cite{wigner1932}. Negativity is not a numerical artifact; it is a structural feature of the Wigner function for any pure state beyond the ground state, and no phase-space rotation or scaling removes it.

The semiclassical limit approaches the problem from the opposite direction. The WKB probability density,
\begin{equation}
P_\text{WKB}(q) \propto \frac{1}{\sqrt{E - V(q)}},
\end{equation}
diverges at the classical turning points $q_\pm$ where $E = V(q)$ and the local momentum vanishes \cite{berry1977,langer1937}. Physically, the particle slows to rest at the boundary; statistically, it spends infinite time there. The divergence is not regularized by any finite order of the WKB expansion.

Both pathologies share a kinematic root. Wigner negativity arises from interference on scales below $\hbar/\Delta p$, and the WKB caustic arises from assuming $\delta q \to 0$ at the turning point --- which demands $\Delta p \to \infty$ and hence unbounded classical action.

\section{The Symplectic Quantum of Action}

De~Gosson maps the Heisenberg limit $\sigma_q \sigma_p \ge \hbar/2$ into classical semi-axes $\Delta q = \sqrt{2}\sigma_q$, $\Delta p = \sqrt{2}\sigma_p$, projecting the $1\sigma$ contour onto a Fermi blob \cite{degosson2012fermi} with phase-space area
\begin{equation}
A = \pi \Delta q \Delta p \ge \frac{h}{2}.
\label{eq:fermi_blob_area}
\end{equation}
Hereafter, $\Delta q$ and $\Delta p$ are classical kinematic boundaries.

Zurek's reciprocal scales \cite{zurek2001} partition the blob interior: $\delta q = \hbar/\Delta p$ and $\delta p = \hbar/\Delta q$. These define a conjugate pair of orthogonal quantum blobs \cite{degosson2009},
\begin{equation}
a_q = \pi\,\delta q\,\Delta p = \frac{h}{2}, \qquad a_p = \pi\,\Delta q\,\delta p = \frac{h}{2},
\end{equation}
each carrying the irreducible area $h/2$. The blobs $a_q$ and $a_p$ are orthogonal in phase space --- $a_q$ squeezed along the position axis and $a_p$ squeezed along the momentum axis \cite{degossoncharlyne2022} --- and their joint structure is accessible only through ensemble tomography \cite{ibort2009} on separate copies of the state \cite{wootterszurek1982}, never through simultaneous measurement of a single copy. Holding their areas fixed while $\Delta q$ and $\Delta p$ expand forces reciprocal compression of $\delta q$ and $\delta p$ --- a topological necessity enforced by Gromov's non-squeezing theorem \cite{gromov1985}. Conjugate pairing locks their areas, $a_q + a_p = h$, to Planck's quantum of action (Fig.~\ref{fig:quantum_of_action}).

\subsection{The Fermi Blob and the Classical Orbit}
\label{sec:fermi_vs_classical}

The Fermi blob $A$ is a property of the \emph{quantum state}, not of the classical orbit. It is read directly from the state's Robertson--Schr\"{o}dinger covariance \cite{robertson1929,schrodinger1930}, with $\Delta q = \sqrt{2\sigma_{qq}}$ and $\Delta p = \sqrt{2\sigma_{pp}}$, and the saturation condition $\sigma_{qq}\sigma_{pp} - \sigma_{qp}^2 = \hbar^2/4$ identifying the minimum-uncertainty case. The blob exists for any quantum state, harmonic or otherwise.

The classical orbit is a different object. The Bohr--Sommerfeld action,
\begin{equation}
A_\text{cl} = \oint p\, dq,
\end{equation}
is the area enclosed by the classical trajectory at the eigenstate's energy. For the harmonic oscillator $A = A_\text{cl}$: the Fermi blob coincides with the energy-shell ellipse. For anharmonic systems the two areas separate, with the quantum state's covariance product $A$ generally smaller than the classical orbit area $A_\text{cl}$.

The kernel widths $\delta q = \hbar/\Delta p$ and $\delta p = \hbar/\Delta q$ are governed by $A$, not $A_\text{cl}$. The state's actual phase-space extent --- not the classical orbit it would trace if it had no quantum spread --- determines the resolution scales.

\section{Resolution via Finite Classical Action}

The classical action $A = \pi \Delta q \Delta p$ sets the phase-space resolution via the reciprocal scales $\delta q = \hbar/\Delta p$ and $\delta p = \hbar/\Delta q$. The minimum-uncertainty blobs $a_q$ and $a_p$ of area $h/2$ take the Gaussian form of the conjugate squeezed kernels \cite{degosson2009,degosson2012fermi},
\begin{equation}
G_{\delta q}(q,p) = \frac{1}{\pi}\exp\!\left(-\frac{q^2}{\delta q^2} - \frac{p^2}{\Delta p^2}\right),
\end{equation}
\begin{equation}
G_{\delta p}(q,p) = \frac{1}{\pi}\exp\!\left(-\frac{q^2}{\Delta q^2} - \frac{p^2}{\delta p^2}\right),
\end{equation}
where $G_{\delta q}$ is squeezed in $\delta q$ and spans $\Delta p$, and $G_{\delta p}$ is squeezed in $\delta p$ and spans $\Delta q$. Each kernel carries symplectic area $h/2$, saturating the Heisenberg limit. The symplectic positivity condition $\delta q \cdot \Delta p = \hbar$ is satisfied by construction from Zurek's reciprocal scales.

The convolved phase-space densities are
\begin{equation}
P_{\delta q}(q,p) = (W * G_{\delta q})(q,p), \qquad P_{\delta p}(q,p) = (W * G_{\delta p})(q,p).
\end{equation}
Since each kernel satisfies the symplectic positivity condition $\lambda_{\min}(\Sigma + \frac{i}{2}J) = 0$, Hudson's theorem \cite{hudson1974} guarantees
\begin{equation}
P_{\delta q}(q,p) \ge 0 \quad \text{and} \quad P_{\delta p}(q,p) \ge 0
\end{equation}
pointwise everywhere in phase space. The finite momentum support of $G_{\delta q}$, with width $\Delta p = \hbar/\delta q$, simultaneously bounds the kinematic action at the classical turning points, replacing WKB caustics with finite-amplitude densities.

The squeezed densities differ fundamentally from the Husimi Q-function \cite{husimi1940}. Husimi convolves $W$ with a fixed coherent-state kernel set once by the ground state, carrying no information about the system's classical action. The kernels $G_{\delta q}$ and $G_{\delta p}$ are governed entirely by $A$: as $A$ grows they sharpen anisotropically along the classical trajectory, recovering phase-space structure the fixed Husimi kernel cannot access.

\section{The Harmonic Oscillator}

The quantum harmonic oscillator provides the canonical test of both resolutions. Its Wigner function is known exactly \cite{wigner1932},
\begin{equation}
W_n(q,p) = \frac{(-1)^n}{\pi}\, e^{-(q^2+p^2)}\, L_n\!\left(2(q^2+p^2)\right),
\end{equation}
where $L_n$ is the Laguerre polynomial of order $n$. The Robertson--Schr\"{o}dinger covariance for eigenstate $|n\rangle$ gives $\sigma_{qq} = \sigma_{pp} = (2n+1)/2$ and $\sigma_{qp} = 0$, yielding classical boundaries $\Delta q = \Delta p = \sqrt{2n+1}$ and reciprocal scales $\delta q = \delta p = 1/\sqrt{2n+1}$, so that $A/A_0 = 2n+1$. Here $A_0 = h/2$ is the ground-state action. Because the harmonic potential is quadratic, $A = A_\text{cl}$ exactly: the Fermi blob coincides with the energy-shell ellipse. All quantities below are expressed in units of the ground-state scales $q_0$ and $p_0$, so that $\hbar = q_0 p_0 = 1$.

\subsection{Wigner Negativity}

Figure~\ref{fig:harmonic_wigner_symplectic} shows the $p=0$ cross-section $W_n(q,0)$ of the exact Wigner function for five action levels, alongside the symplectic resolution $P_{\delta q}(q,0)$. For $n=0$ the cross-section is a positive Gaussian --- the ground state Wigner function is non-negative everywhere in phase space. For $n \ge 1$ the cross-section oscillates and goes negative, with $W_n(0,0) = (-1)^n/\pi$ alternating sign at the origin. The dark-shaded negative regions grow in extent with increasing $n$, reflecting deepening quantum interference.

The convolved cross-section $P_{\delta q}(q,0)$ in the right column is everywhere non-negative, consistent with Hudson's theorem guaranteeing $P_{\delta q}(q,p) \ge 0$ pointwise throughout phase space. The kernel width $\delta q = 1/\sqrt{2n+1}$ shrinks with increasing action, so at $n=20$ ($A = 41\,A_0$) the convolution preserves nearly all of the Wigner oscillation structure while still eliminating all negativity. Each row is independently scaled; the absolute amplitude of the cross-section decreases with $n$ as the distribution spreads over the growing classical region.

\section{Morse Potential}

The Morse oscillator $V(q) = D_e(1 - e^{-\alpha q})^2$ provides an anharmonic test where the Fermi blob and the classical orbit no longer coincide. We use $D_e = 30$, $\alpha = 1$, $m = 1$, supporting eight bound states. For each bound state the quantum covariance product $A$ is smaller than the Bohr--Sommerfeld action $A_\text{cl}$, with the gap widening as $n$ approaches dissociation. The kernel widths $\delta q$ and $\delta p$ are sized by $A$, the state's actual phase-space cell, not by the larger classical envelope.

Figure~\ref{fig:morse_wigner_symplectic} shows three states: the ground state ($n=0$), an intermediate anharmonic state ($n=8$), and a strongly anharmonic state ($n=16$). With $\omega_0 = 1$, the ground state has $\Delta q = \Delta p = 1$ to within numerical precision and the Fermi blob $A$ is circular --- coinciding with the harmonic oscillator ground state. As $n$ grows, the orbit develops the characteristic horseshoe shape of Morse confinement; $\Delta q$ stretches as the wavefunction reaches further into the soft-wall region while $\Delta p$ remains bounded by the dissociation energy. The symplectic kernel $G_{\delta q}$ inherits this anisotropy, sharpening preferentially along the classical trajectory. The convolved cross-section $P_{\delta q}(q,0)$ retains the multi-lobed structure of $W_n(q,0)$ while remaining non-negative everywhere, in contrast to the Husimi resolution (Appendix, Fig.~\ref{fig:morse_wigner_husimi}) which collapses the interior oscillations to a single peak at the slow turning point.

\section{Double-Well Potential}

The symmetric double-well $V(q) = \mu^2(q^2 - V_0)^2/(4 V_0) - \mu^2 V_0/4$ with $\mu^2 = 4$, $V_0 = 4$ supports tunneling doublets below the barrier and connected orbits above it. For doublet partners the classical envelope is the union of two disconnected orbits, but the quantum state's covariance is dominated by delocalization across both wells, yielding a single Fermi blob $A$ that envelops the entire double-well region. The Fermi blob captures the coherent superposition that the disconnected classical picture cannot.

Figure~\ref{fig:double_well_wigner_symplectic} demonstrates the most stringent test of the resolution: the inter-well interference peak in $W_0(q,0)$ that is the phase-space signature of coherent superposition between the two wells. This central peak is preserved by the symplectic kernel because the kernel width $\delta q = \hbar/\Delta p$ is small enough to resolve sub-well features when the state's momentum spread $\Delta p$ is large. The fixed-width Husimi kernel (Appendix, Fig.~\ref{fig:double_well_wigner_husimi}) flattens this central peak entirely.

\section{Discussion}

Wigner negativity and WKB caustics are not separate pathologies of separate formalisms but two manifestations of the same kinematic assumption: that classical action is unbounded. Enforcing the finite classical action $A$ of a physical system via Zurek's reciprocal scales $\delta q = \hbar/\Delta p$ and $\delta p = \hbar/\Delta q$ produces a conjugate pair of squeezed Gaussian kernels $G_{\delta q}$ and $G_{\delta p}$, each of phase-space area $h/2$, summing to the quantum of action $h$. Convolution with either kernel yields a phase-space density that is provably non-negative by Hudson's theorem and finite at classical turning points by construction.

Across three potentials --- harmonic, Morse, and double-well --- the symplectic resolution preserves quantum oscillation structure that the fixed-width Husimi Q-function destroys. The improvement is most dramatic for high-action states where Husimi's ground-state-sized kernel over-smooths by orders of magnitude, and for tunneling doublets where the inter-well interference peak is the diagnostic of quantum coherence.

The construction introduces no free parameters: $\Delta q$ and $\Delta p$ are read directly from the state's Robertson--Schr\"{o}dinger covariance, and $\delta q$, $\delta p$ are determined by the Heisenberg limit. The kernel adapts to each state by the same rule that makes the state quantum-mechanical in the first place. The quantum of action does the work; we have only to use it.
