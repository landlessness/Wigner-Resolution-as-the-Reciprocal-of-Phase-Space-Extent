\begin{abstract}

\section{The Symplectic Quantum of Action}

De Gosson \cite{degosson2009} geometrically characterizes the statistical Heisenberg limit, $\sigma_q \sigma_p \ge \hbar/2$, via classical semi-axes $\Delta q = \sqrt{2}\sigma_q$ and $\Delta p = \sqrt{2}\sigma_p$, establishing the macroscopic action area:
$$A = \pi \Delta q \Delta p \ge \frac{h}{2}.$$

The interior of this Hamiltonian boundary is structured by Zurek's reciprocal phase space scales \cite{zurek2001}. Rooted in quantum metrology \cite{caves1981}, spatial resolution is funded by the conjugate momentum spread, $\delta q = \hbar / \Delta p$, and vice versa, $\delta p = \hbar / \Delta q$. 

Expressed as phase space areas, these metrological bounds form a pair of centered, orthogonal ellipses:
$$ a_q = \pi \delta q \Delta p = \frac{h}{2} \quad \text{and} \quad a_p = \pi \Delta q \delta p = \frac{h}{2}. $$ 
These are precisely a conjugate pair of de Gosson's minimum-uncertainty quantum blobs.

We therefore identify the quantum of action (see Fig.~\ref{fig:quantum_of_action}) as the sum of this conjugate pair:
$$h = a_q + a_p.$$
This reframes Planck's constant from an indivisible cell of phase space into a dynamic, symplectic form.

In the ground state, the blobs degenerate into a perfect circle where $A = a_q = a_p = h/2$ (see Fig.~\ref{fig:qho_grid}, left column, top row). As $A$ increases, this symplectic exchange compresses the quantum blobs under the topological constraint of Gromov's non-squeezing theorem \cite{gromov1985}, shrinking $a_q$ and $a_p$ and geometrically enforcing the correspondence principle.

While each blob upholds the invariant quantum floor ($h/2$), the squeezing via classical action $A$ collapses the orthogonal blobs $a_q$ and $a_p$ into fine crosshairs (see Fig.~\ref{fig:qho_grid}, left column, bottom row), forbidding macroscopic uncertainty.

\section{Semiclassical Singularities}

While the semiclassical WKB approximation \cite{heller2018} successfully bridges classical orbits with discrete quantum states, its spatial probability density fails at the physical boundaries. The classical spatial probability density $P_{\text{cl}}(q)$ is inversely proportional to the classical momentum $p(q)$:
\begin{equation}
P_{\text{cl}}(q) \propto \frac{1}{p(q)} = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2m(E - V(q))}}.
\end{equation}

At the turning points where $E = V(q)$, the momentum vanishes ($p \to 0$), and the projection onto the coordinate axis exhibits a caustic divergence:
\begin{equation}
\lim_{q \to q_t} P_{\text{cl}}(q) = \infty.
\end{equation}

Furthermore, the approximation suffers a qualitative failure in the ground-state limit. While the exact quantum density $|\psi_0(q)|^2$ is a central Gaussian maximum, the classical density $P_{\text{cl}}(q)$ exhibits a local minimum at the potential center. For the harmonic oscillator, the second derivative is positive at the origin:
\begin{equation}
\left. \frac{d^2 P_{\text{cl}}}{dq^2} \right|_{q=0} = \frac{m\omega^2}{2E} P_{\text{cl}}(0) > 0.
\end{equation}

This resulting unphysical ``saddle'' represents a fundamental breakdown of the 1D semiclassical approximation. Bridging these classical boundaries with finite quantum observables requires a mechanism that accounts for the irreducible symplectic area of the state.


\section{Symplectic Resolution}

To resolve these singularities, we deploy the symplectic quantum of action, $h = a_q + a_p$, as a symplectic kernel $K_h$. We map the divergent 1D projection $P_{\text{cl}}(q)$ to a physically realizable quantum distribution $P_{\text{qm}}(q)$ by convolving it with the conjugate phase-space support $\delta q$ of the quantum blob $a_q$.

Because the spatial resolution $\delta q$ is bounded by the reciprocal momentum, $\delta q = h / (2\pi \Delta p)$, we define the symplectic kernel $K_h$ as the normalized Gaussian profile of this geometric area. Substituting for $\delta q$ reveals Planck's quantum of action, $h$, explicitly regulating the distribution:
$$K_h(q) = \frac{2\sqrt{\pi} \Delta p}{h} \exp\left( - \left( \frac{2\pi \Delta p \, q}{h} \right)^2 \right).$$

The physical density $P_{\text{qm}}(q)$ is obtained through the convolution of the classical density $P_{\text{cl}}(q)$ with this symplectic kernel $K_h$:
$$P_{\text{qm}}(q) = (P_{\text{cl}} * K_h)(q) = \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} P_{\text{cl}}(q') K_h(q - q') \, dq'.$$

Although the classical density diverges at the turning points, the singularity scales as $1/\sqrt{q}$, meaning the localized probability mass remains finite. Kinematically, sustaining an infinite spatial density requires compressing the conjugate momentum width to zero—a geometric collapse that demands infinite action. 

Because the available action is strictly limited to the finite quantum $h$, this infinite collapse is physically forbidden. Integrating the semiclassical singularities against the symplectic kernel $K_h$ distributes the finite probability mass across the $\delta q$ width of the quantum blob. The explicit presence of $h$ structurally prevents the kinematic collapse, transforming the non-physical caustics into finite, observable probability amplitudes.

\section{The Harmonic Oscillator}

We demonstrate this geometric resolution via the harmonic oscillator (see Fig.~\ref{fig:qho_grid}). Following Berry's formulation of semiclassical mechanics \cite{berry1977}, the 2D Wigner phase-space distribution is governed by the geometric area of chords cutting across the classical action boundary. For any phase-space point $(q,p)$ interior to the classical trajectory, the amplitude is determined by constructing a straight chord bisected by $(q,p)$ with endpoints resting on the energy contour. The semiclassical Wigner distribution oscillates according to the geometric phase-space area $A_{\text{chord}}(q,p)$ bounded between this chord and the classical trajectory:
$$W(q,p) \propto \cos\left( \frac{A_{\text{chord}}(q,p)}{\hbar} - \frac{\pi}{4} \right).$$

Classical caustics emerge as geometric artifacts when this 2D distribution is projected onto the 1D spatial axis, an operation that implicitly treats the classical boundary as a perfectly sharp 1D curve.

Rather than applying Airy functions over the turning points to smooth the mathematics, we execute a 2D symplectic projection. The raw geometric density is integrated against the spatial width $\delta q$ of the symplectic crosshair. Because a physical state cannot be localized to a 1D trajectory without requiring infinite conjugate momentum variance, it maintains an irreducible symplectic area ($a_q = h/2$). This inherent phase-space dispersion forbids the spatial projection from reaching zero width. The $\delta q$ scale of the symplectic kernel $K_h$ prevents the momentum support from collapsing, bounding the caustic divergence and yielding the finite probability amplitudes characteristic of discrete quantum states.
