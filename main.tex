\title{Resolving Wigner Negativity and Semiclassical Caustics via Classical Action}

\begin{abstract}
Wigner negativity and semiclassical caustics both follow from assuming unbounded classical action. Enforcing the finite action of a physical system via symplectic phase-space cells resolves both simultaneously. The resulting phase-space densities are provably non-negative and free of turning-point divergences.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}

Phase-space quantum mechanics has lived with two stubborn pathologies for nearly a century. The Wigner--Weyl distribution, an exact phase-space representation of any quantum state, takes negative values wherever interference is strong \cite{wigner1932}. The WKB probability density, the leading-order semiclassical approximation, diverges at every classical turning point \cite{berry1977,langer1937}. Each is treated as an inconvenience of its own formalism.

They are the same problem. Both arise from assuming that classical action is unbounded --- an assumption no physical system satisfies. The standard remedy, the Husimi Q-function \cite{husimi1940}, blurs both away with a Gaussian kernel set once by the ground state. But that kernel knows nothing about the system at hand: it cannot resolve structure governed by an action it never sees.

Here we replace the Husimi kernel with one whose width is fixed by the state's own classical action. The construction is forced: de~Gosson's symplectic geometry \cite{degosson2009,degosson2012fermi}, Zurek's reciprocal scales \cite{zurek2001}, and Gromov's non-squeezing theorem \cite{gromov1985} together pick out a unique pair of squeezed Gaussian kernels. Hudson's theorem \cite{hudson1974} guarantees the resulting density is everywhere non-negative; finite momentum support guarantees it is everywhere finite. One construction resolves both pathologies, and across three test potentials it preserves the quantum oscillation structure that the Husimi kernel destroys.

\section{Wigner Negativity and Semiclassical Caustics}

The Wigner--Weyl distribution furnishes an exact phase-space representation of a quantum state $\psi$,
\begin{equation}
W(q,p) = \frac{1}{\pi\hbar}\int_{-\infty}^{\infty} \psi^*(q+x)\psi(q-x)\, e^{2ipx/\hbar}\, dx,
\end{equation}
but allows negative values wherever quantum interference is strong \cite{wigner1932}. Negativity is not a numerical artifact; it is a structural feature of the Wigner function for any pure state beyond the ground state, and no phase-space rotation or scaling removes it.

The semiclassical limit approaches the problem from the opposite direction. The WKB probability density,
\begin{equation}
P_\text{WKB}(q) = \frac{1}{T(E)\sqrt{2(E - V(q))}},
\end{equation}
diverges at the classical turning points $q_\pm$ where $E = V(q)$ and the local momentum vanishes \cite{berry1977,langer1937}. Here $T(E)$ is the orbit period; physically, the particle slows to rest at the boundary, and statistically it spends infinite time there. The divergence is not regularized by any finite order of the WKB expansion.

Both pathologies share a kinematic root. Wigner negativity arises from interference on scales below $\hbar/\Delta p$, and the WKB caustic arises from assuming $\delta q \to 0$ at the turning point --- which demands $\Delta p \to \infty$ and hence unbounded classical action.

\section{The Symplectic Quantum of Action}

De~Gosson maps the Heisenberg limit $\sigma_q \sigma_p \ge \hbar/2$ into classical semi-axes $\Delta q = \sqrt{2}\sigma_q$, $\Delta p = \sqrt{2}\sigma_p$, projecting the $1\sigma$ contour onto a Fermi blob \cite{degosson2012fermi} with phase-space area
\begin{equation}
A = \pi \Delta q \Delta p \ge \frac{h}{2}.
\label{eq:fermi_blob_area}
\end{equation}
Hereafter, $\Delta q$ and $\Delta p$ are classical kinematic boundaries.

Zurek's reciprocal scales \cite{zurek2001} partition the blob interior: $\delta q = \hbar/\Delta p$ and $\delta p = \hbar/\Delta q$. These define a conjugate pair of orthogonal quantum blobs \cite{degosson2009},
\begin{equation}
a_q = \pi\,\delta q\,\Delta p = \frac{h}{2}, \qquad a_p = \pi\,\Delta q\,\delta p = \frac{h}{2},
\end{equation}
each carrying the irreducible area $h/2$. The blobs $a_q$ and $a_p$ are orthogonal in phase space --- $a_q$ squeezed along the position axis and $a_p$ squeezed along the momentum axis \cite{degossoncharlyne2022} --- and their joint structure is accessible only through ensemble tomography \cite{ibort2009} on separate copies of the state \cite{wootterszurek1982}, never through simultaneous measurement of a single copy. Holding their areas fixed while $\Delta q$ and $\Delta p$ expand forces reciprocal compression of $\delta q$ and $\delta p$ --- a topological necessity enforced by Gromov's non-squeezing theorem \cite{gromov1985}. Conjugate pairing locks their areas, $a_q + a_p = h$, to Planck's quantum of action (Fig.~\ref{fig:quantum_of_action}).

\subsection{The Fermi Blob and the Classical Orbit}
\label{sec:fermi_vs_classical}

The Fermi blob $A$ is a property of the \emph{quantum state}, not of the classical orbit. It is read directly from the state's Robertson--Schr\"{o}dinger covariance \cite{robertson1929,schrodinger1930}, with $\Delta q = \sqrt{2\sigma_{qq}}$ and $\Delta p = \sqrt{2\sigma_{pp}}$, and the saturation condition $\sigma_{qq}\sigma_{pp} - \sigma_{qp}^2 = \hbar^2/4$ identifying the minimum-uncertainty case. The blob exists for any quantum state, harmonic or otherwise.

The classical orbit is a different object. The Bohr--Sommerfeld action,
\begin{equation}
A_\text{cl} = \oint p\, dq,
\end{equation}
is the area enclosed by the classical trajectory at the eigenstate's energy. For the harmonic oscillator $A = A_\text{cl}$: the Fermi blob coincides with the energy-shell ellipse. For anharmonic systems the two areas separate, with the quantum state's covariance product $A$ generally smaller than the classical orbit area $A_\text{cl}$.

The kernel widths $\delta q = \hbar/\Delta p$ and $\delta p = \hbar/\Delta q$ are governed by $A$, not $A_\text{cl}$. The state's actual phase-space extent --- not the classical orbit it would trace if it had no quantum spread --- determines the resolution scales.

\section{Resolution via Finite Classical Action}

The classical action $A = \pi \Delta q \Delta p$ sets the phase-space resolution via the reciprocal scales $\delta q = \hbar/\Delta p$ and $\delta p = \hbar/\Delta q$. De Gosson's minimum-uncertainty blobs $a_q$ and $a_p$ of area $h/2$ take the Gaussian form of the conjugate squeezed kernels \cite{degosson2009,degosson2012fermi},
\begin{equation}
G_{\delta q}(q,p) = \frac{1}{\pi}\exp\!\left(-\frac{q^2}{\delta q^2} - \frac{p^2}{\Delta p^2}\right),
\end{equation}
\begin{equation}
G_{\delta p}(q,p) = \frac{1}{\pi}\exp\!\left(-\frac{q^2}{\Delta q^2} - \frac{p^2}{\delta p^2}\right),
\end{equation}
where $G_{\delta q}$ is squeezed in $\delta q$ and spans $\Delta p$, and $G_{\delta p}$ is squeezed in $\delta p$ and spans $\Delta q$. Each kernel carries symplectic area $h/2$, saturating the Heisenberg limit. The symplectic positivity condition $\delta q \cdot \Delta p = \hbar$ is satisfied by construction from Zurek's reciprocal scales.

The convolved phase-space densities are
\begin{equation}
P_{\delta q}(q,p) = (W * G_{\delta q})(q,p), \qquad P_{\delta p}(q,p) = (W * G_{\delta p})(q,p).
\end{equation}
Since each kernel satisfies the symplectic positivity condition $\lambda_{\min}(\Sigma + \frac{i}{2}J) = 0$, Hudson's theorem \cite{hudson1974} guarantees
\begin{equation}
P_{\delta q}(q,p) \ge 0 \quad \text{and} \quad P_{\delta p}(q,p) \ge 0
\end{equation}
pointwise everywhere in phase space. The finite momentum support of $G_{\delta q}$, with width $\Delta p = \hbar/\delta q$, simultaneously bounds the kinematic action at the classical turning points, replacing WKB caustics with finite-amplitude densities. The 1D position density obtained by marginalizing the convolved 2D shell over $p$,
\begin{equation}
\rho_{\delta q}(q) = \int (W_\text{cl} * G_{\delta q})(q,p)\, dp,
\end{equation}
where $W_\text{cl}(q,p) \propto \delta(H(q,p) - E)$ is the classical microcanonical density, is everywhere finite even where $P_\text{WKB}(q)$ diverges.

The squeezed densities differ fundamentally from the Husimi Q-function \cite{husimi1940}. Husimi convolves $W$ with a fixed coherent-state kernel set once by the ground state, carrying no information about the system's classical action. The kernels $G_{\delta q}$ and $G_{\delta p}$ are governed entirely by $A$: as $A$ grows they sharpen anisotropically along the classical trajectory, recovering phase-space structure the fixed Husimi kernel cannot access.

\section{Numerical Demonstrations}

The construction above is analytical: Hudson's theorem guarantees pointwise non-negativity of $P_{\delta q}$ and $P_{\delta p}$ for any state, and finite momentum support guarantees the classical caustic is replaced by a finite peak whatever the potential. The numerical demonstrations that follow do not test these guarantees; they show what the resolved densities actually look like across three potentials chosen to cover progressively more demanding quantum behavior. The harmonic oscillator anchors the construction in a system where the Fermi blob and the classical orbit coincide. The Morse potential breaks that coincidence, separating the quantum action $A$ from the Bohr--Sommerfeld action $A_\text{cl}$. The double-well introduces tunneling doublets, where the classical orbit is disconnected but the quantum state is not. Across all three, both Wigner and semiclassical input are resolved by the same kernel.

\subsection{The Harmonic Oscillator}

The quantum harmonic oscillator provides the canonical test of both resolutions. Its Wigner function is known exactly \cite{wigner1932},
\begin{equation}
W_n(q,p) = \frac{(-1)^n}{\pi}\, e^{-(q^2+p^2)}\, L_n\!\left(2(q^2+p^2)\right),
\end{equation}
where $L_n$ is the Laguerre polynomial of order $n$. The Robertson--Schr\"{o}dinger covariance for eigenstate $|n\rangle$ gives $\sigma_{qq} = \sigma_{pp} = (2n+1)/2$ and $\sigma_{qp} = 0$, yielding classical boundaries $\Delta q = \Delta p = \sqrt{2n+1}$ and reciprocal scales $\delta q = \delta p = 1/\sqrt{2n+1}$, so that $A/A_0 = 2n+1$. Here $A_0 = h/2$ is the ground-state action. Because the harmonic potential is quadratic, $A = A_\text{cl}$ exactly: the Fermi blob coincides with the energy-shell ellipse. All quantities below are expressed in units of the ground-state scales $q_0$ and $p_0$, so that $\hbar = q_0 p_0 = 1$.

Figure~\ref{fig:harmonic_semiclassical_symplectic} shows the parallel semiclassical picture for the same five action levels. The center column displays the analytical WKB density, which diverges as $1/\sqrt{E - V(q)}$ at every classical turning point. The divergences are not visualization choices but genuine mathematical singularities: the classical density of states is infinite where momentum vanishes.

The right column shows the symplectic resolution $\rho_{\delta q}(q)$, obtained by marginalizing the convolution $W_\text{cl} * G_{\delta q}$ over $p$. The kernel's finite momentum support $\Delta p = \sqrt{2n+1}$ bounds the kinematic action at the turning points, replacing each divergence with a finite peak. As $n$ grows the resolution evolves from a smooth bell ($n=0$, where the kernel width $\delta q = 1$ exceeds the orbit half-width $\Delta q = 1$) toward two sharp turning-point peaks bracketing a relatively flat interior ($n=20$). The semiclassical limit emerges as the resolved density tracks the classical $1/p(q)$ profile away from the turning points, while remaining finite at them.

\subsection{Morse Potential}

The Morse oscillator $V(q) = D_e(1 - e^{-\alpha q})^2$ provides an anharmonic test where the Fermi blob and the classical orbit no longer coincide. We use $D_e = 12.5$, $\alpha = 0.2$, $m = 1$, chosen so that $\omega_0 = \alpha\sqrt{2D_e/m} = 1$ matches the harmonic oscillator's natural frequency. For each bound state the quantum covariance product $A$ is smaller than the Bohr--Sommerfeld action $A_\text{cl}$, with the gap widening as $n$ approaches dissociation. The kernel widths $\delta q$ and $\delta p$ are sized by $A$, the state's actual phase-space cell, not by the larger classical envelope.

Where the harmonic-oscillator caustic was symmetric, the Morse caustic is dramatically lopsided: the classical particle spends much longer near the soft-wall turning point than the steep-wall one, and the WKB density correspondingly accumulates more probability there. Both features --- the asymmetry and the divergences --- are preserved by the symplectic kernel, which adapts its anisotropy to the state's classical envelope while bounding the divergences via $\Delta p$.

\subsection{Double-Well Potential}

The symmetric double-well $V(q) = -\mu^2 q^2/2 + \lambda q^4/4$ with $\mu^2 = 4$, $\lambda = 1$ supports tunneling doublets below the barrier and connected orbits above it. Here $V_0 \equiv \mu^4/(4\lambda) = 4$ is the barrier height and $\pm\sqrt{\mu^2/\lambda} = \pm 2$ are the well minima. For doublet partners the classical envelope is the union of two disconnected orbits, but the quantum state's covariance is dominated by delocalization across both wells, yielding a single Fermi blob $A$ that envelops the entire double-well region. The Fermi blob captures the coherent superposition that the disconnected classical picture cannot.

Both pictures together show the kernel honestly represents whatever density structure it convolves.

\section{Discussion}

Wigner negativity and WKB caustics are not separate pathologies of separate formalisms but two manifestations of the same kinematic assumption: that classical action is unbounded. Enforcing the finite classical action $A$ of a physical system via Zurek's reciprocal scales $\delta q = \hbar/\Delta p$ and $\delta p = \hbar/\Delta q$ produces a conjugate pair of squeezed Gaussian kernels $G_{\delta q}$ and $G_{\delta p}$, each of phase-space area $h/2$, summing to the quantum of action $h$. Convolution with either kernel yields a phase-space density that is provably non-negative by Hudson's theorem and finite at classical turning points by construction.

Across three potentials --- harmonic, Morse, and double-well --- the symplectic resolution preserves quantum oscillation structure that the fixed-width Husimi Q-function destroys. The improvement is most dramatic for high-action states where Husimi's ground-state-sized kernel over-smooths by orders of magnitude, and for tunneling doublets where the inter-well interference peak is the diagnostic of quantum coherence. For semiclassical caustics, the same kernel replaces the WKB turning-point divergence with a finite peak whose width is set by $\Delta p$ and whose location matches the classical turning point.

The construction introduces no free parameters: $\Delta q$ and $\Delta p$ are read directly from the state's Robertson--Schr\"{o}dinger covariance, and $\delta q$, $\delta p$ follow from de~Gosson's quantum blobs at the Heisenberg limit. The kernel adapts to each state by the same rule that makes the state quantum-mechanical in the first place. Wigner negativity and semiclassical caustics are resolved via classical action.
