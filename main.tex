\title{Wigner Resolution as the Reciprocal of Phase-Space Extent}

\begin{abstract}
The Heisenberg principle bounds a quantum state's outer extent in phase space. A complementary inner scale, set by the same quantum of action, is reached independently by two routes: de Gosson's symplectic polar duality of the covariance ellipsoid, and Zurek's interference-driven sub-Planck chessboard. Both arrive at resolutions that are reciprocals of the phase space extent. Convolution of the Wigner function with the squeezed-coherent kernel at this scale yields a non-negative density determined by the state itself. Established non-negative phase-space methods---truncated Wigner approximations, generalized Husimi smoothings, maximum-likelihood reconstructions, Cahill--Glauber distributions---converge on this same scale in their respective regimes of success. We give the scale its symplectic-geometric origin.
\end{abstract}

The Wigner function~\cite{wigner1932} is the standard joint phase-space representation of a quantum state. For non-Gaussian states it takes negative values~\cite{hudson1974}. Many working contexts call for non-negative phase-space densities, and several construction routes have proven successful in practice. Truncated Wigner approximations~\cite{polkovnikov2010} sample classical trajectories and produce accurate semiclassical dynamics when the sampling matches the state's covariance. Husimi-style smoothings with state-dependent kernels~\cite{wodkiewicz1984, wuensche1995} reconstruct positive densities from generalized $P_{\lambda\theta}$ representations when $\lambda$ is matched to the state's spread. Maximum-likelihood reconstruction with Fock-state truncation produces non-negative reconstructions when the truncation is matched to the state's available action. Cahill--Glauber $W^{(s)}$ distributions~\cite{cahillglauber1969} recover positivity when the ordering parameter $s$ is matched to the state's covariance. Each method names its own kernel, ordering, or cutoff in the language of its subfield. The same scale appears in each: the reciprocal of the state's phase-space extent. We give this scale its symplectic-geometric origin and derive the smoothing it defines.

% ===============================================================================
%  SLICE 1, PIECE B --- de Gosson
% ===============================================================================

A quantum state occupies phase space\footnote{Heisenberg cells are conventionally drawn as rectangles for visual simplicity; the correct symplectic-geometric object is an ellipsoid~\cite{degosson2013qblobs, degossoncharlyne2022}. Throughout this Letter, ``cell'' denotes a de Gosson ellipsoid.}, with extent set by its covariance: an extended cell, the phase-space ellipsoid with semi-axes $\Delta x = \sqrt{2}\sigma_x$, $\Delta p = \sqrt{2}\sigma_p$ and action
\begin{equation}
A = \pi\,\Delta x\,\Delta p \ge h/2,
\label{eq:extended_cell}
\end{equation}
floored at $h/2$ by Heisenberg~\cite{robertson1929,schrodinger1930}. De Gosson identifies a complementary cell inscribed in the extended one~\cite{degosson2013qblobs, degossoncharlyne2022}: the squeezed cell, of action exactly $h/2$,
\begin{equation}
a = \pi\,\delta_\parallel\,\delta_\perp = h/2,
\label{eq:squeezed_cell}
\end{equation}
with semi-axes $\delta_\parallel$ and $\delta_\perp$ tied by the polar-dual identity $\delta_\parallel \cdot \delta_\perp = \hbar$ and bounded by the inscription within the extended cell. Theorem~13 of \cite{degossoncharlyne2022} establishes that an inscribed cell carries action $h/2$ exactly when the spreads it shows along orthogonal directions are reciprocals. Gromov's non-squeezing theorem~\cite{gromov1985} forbids any canonical compression below $h/2$.

% ===============================================================================
%  SLICE 1, PIECE C --- tomography
% ===============================================================================

The state fixes the extended cell. As $\theta$ varies, the inscribed squeezed cell pivots within and its semi-axes flow according to
\begin{equation}
\delta_\parallel(\theta) = \hbar\sqrt{ \frac{\cos^2\theta}{\Delta p^2} + \frac{\sin^2\theta}{\Delta x^2} }, \qquad \delta_\perp(\theta) = \frac{\hbar}{\delta_\parallel(\theta)},
\label{eq:cell_axes_theta}
\end{equation}
tied by the polar-dual identity~\cite{degossoncharlyne2022}. The shape tracks $\theta$; the action stays at $h/2$. The cells $a_x$ and $a_p$ of Fig.~\ref{fig:zurek_cells} sit with axes along $x$ and $p$, at $\theta = 0$ and $\theta = \pi/2$; the rotated cell of Fig.~\ref{fig:de_gosson_cells} illustrates a typical orientation. The same rotated axis labels the quadrature in continuous-variable tomography~\cite{vogelrisken1989, lvovsky2009}.

% ===============================================================================
%  SLICE 1, PIECE D --- Zurek
% ===============================================================================
Zurek discovered that the Wigner function carries interference structure on sub-Planck scales~\cite{zurek2001}. With the state's extended cell $A$ providing the spreads $\Delta x$ and $\Delta p$, the smallest interference periods are
\begin{equation}
\delta_x = \hbar/\Delta p, \qquad \delta_p = \hbar/\Delta x.
\label{eq:zurek}
\end{equation}
These interference scales are the principal-axis values of $\delta_\parallel$ and $\delta_\perp$: de Gosson's polar-dual semi-axes when the squeezed cell aligns with the $x$ and $p$ axes. Zurek shows that they control the state's sensitivity to phase-space displacements, and hence its decoherence rate.

% ===============================================================================
%  SLICE 1, PIECE E --- Convergence
% ===============================================================================
Two physical pictures---the cell that cannot be squeezed below the action $h/2$, and the chessboard of sub-Planck interference---reach the same scales. This convergence is the starting point of this Letter.

Prior work has touched the same convergence from adjacent directions. Dragoman, working in the phase-space formulation of quantum mechanics, argued that an apparatus produces a non-negative density when its resolution matches the state's spread~\cite{dragoman2005}---the same matching condition we derive here from symplectic geometry. Cordero, de Gosson, D\"orfler, and Nicola studied Born--Jordan distributions of Gaussian superpositions in the ``diamond'' configuration and connected de Gosson's symplectic-covariance program directly to Zurek's sub-Planck interference structure~\cite{cordero2018}. This Letter gives the convergence its geometric origin and derives the smoothing it defines.

% ======================================================================
% SLICE 2 --- THE DENSITY
% ======================================================================

The Wigner--Weyl distribution~\cite{wigner1932}
\begin{equation}
W(x,p) = \frac{1}{\pi\hbar}\int \psi^*(x+y)\,\psi(x-y)\,e^{2ipy/\hbar}\, dy
\label{eq:wigner}
\end{equation}
takes negative values for any non-Gaussian state~\cite{hudson1974}. By \eqref{eq:zurek} the pointwise limit $\delta_\parallel \to 0$ requires $\delta_\perp \to \infty$ and unbounded covariance area: pointwise sampling of $W$ asks a finer question than the de Gosson cells permit.

The squeezed-coherent kernel matched to the state's covariance,
\begin{equation}
G_\theta(x,p) = \frac{1}{\pi}\exp\!\left(-\frac{x_\theta^2}{\delta_\parallel^2} - \frac{p_\theta^2}{\delta_\perp^2}\right),
\label{eq:Gtheta}
\end{equation}
where $x_\theta = x\cos\theta + p\sin\theta$ and $p_\theta = -x\sin\theta + p\cos\theta$ are the rotated coordinates, is the Wigner transform of the squeezed coherent state at the state's covariance~\cite{degossoncharlyne2022}, of area exactly $h/2$. The convolution
\begin{equation}
P_\theta(x,p) = (W * G_\theta)(x,p)
\label{eq:Ptheta}
\end{equation}
is non-negative everywhere, since convolution with any quantum-cell Wigner function yields a non-negative distribution~\cite{degosson2005bullsci,degosson2013qblobs}. The kernel is fixed by the state's covariance and the orientation $\theta$; no free parameter.

The polar-dual smoothing $P_\theta$ produces a non-negative phase-space density at a resolution determined by the state itself. The resolution is geometric: it follows from de Gosson's symplectic polar duality and matches Zurek's interference scales at principal-axis orientations. It coincides with the resolution at which established non-negative phase-space methods produce correct results.

The convergence across methods rooted in different premises---semiclassical sampling, ordering conventions, vacuum reference, Hilbert-space cutoffs---suggests that the polar-dual scale is a universal feature of phase-space resolution rather than an artifact of any particular construction. The geometric framework gives the convergence a unified origin in symplectic structure.
