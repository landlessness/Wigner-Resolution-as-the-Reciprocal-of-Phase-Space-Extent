\title{Wigner Resolution as the Reciprocal of Action}

\begin{abstract}
A quantum state carries a phase-space action of at least Planck's quantum. As its extent grows along one direction, its conjugate scale contracts in inverse proportion, with the product held at the quantum of action. The Wigner function of the state shows fringes at this conjugate scale, the interference between classical paths separated by less than a quantum of action. At any finer resolution the fringes dip below zero. At the conjugate scale itself they sit within the action the state carries, and the density is everywhere non-negative. Zurek's sub-Planck features are this conjugate scale aligned with position and momentum, the limiting case of a continuous family of resolutions tied to the action of the state. Truncated Wigner approximation, generalized Husimi smoothing, maximum-likelihood reconstruction, and Cahill--Glauber ordering each succeed at this same conjugate scale. Wigner resolution is the reciprocal of action.
\end{abstract}

% ===============================================================================
%  OPENING --- Wigner negativity is real, and so is the need for positive densities
% ===============================================================================

The Wigner function~\cite{wigner1932} is the phase-space portrait of a quantum state. Its positive regions correspond to classical configurations of the state, and the regions where it dips below zero~\cite{hudson1974} are the interference between alternative classical paths. The negative regions quantify non-classicality~\cite{kenfack2004} and certify resources for continuous-variable quantum computation~\cite{marieisert2012,veitch2012}.

Some tasks require a phase-space density that can be read as a classical probability. Semiclassical sampling, tomographic reconstruction, and classical-limit transport all proceed this way. The interference fringes must then be unresolved, leaving a non-negative density. Several methods do this. Each names its own parameter. The parameter may be a sampling width, a kernel scale, a Fock cutoff, or an ordering. Each method works when its parameter is matched to the state's extent. This Letter identifies the physical origin of that matching condition. The matching scale is set by the action of the state.

We work in one degree of freedom and align the coordinates with the principal axes of the state's covariance ellipse. The Robertson--Schr\"odinger covariance term~\cite{robertson1929,schrodinger1930} then vanishes, leaving the position spread $\Delta x = \sqrt{2}\sigma_x$ and the momentum spread $\Delta p = \sqrt{2}\sigma_p$. The symplectic results we use~\cite{degosson2013qblobs,degossoncharlyne2022} extend without modification to higher dimensions and to the full off-diagonal covariance. The state's extent in phase space is its \emph{extended cell} (Fig.~\ref{fig:de_gosson_cells}, outer ellipse), an ellipse of action area
\begin{equation}
A = \pi\,\Delta x\,\Delta p \ge h/2,
\label{eq:extended_cell}
\end{equation}
floored at the quantum of action by Heisenberg. De Gosson identifies the smallest phase-space region the state can locally occupy, the \emph{squeezed cell}~\cite{degosson2013qblobs,degossoncharlyne2022}, an ellipse of fixed action
\begin{equation}
a = \pi\,\delta_\parallel\,\delta_\perp = h/2,
\label{eq:squeezed_cell}
\end{equation}
parameterized by orientation $\theta$, with semi-axes tied by $\delta_\parallel\,\delta_\perp = \hbar$. Gromov's non-squeezing theorem~\cite{gromov1985} forbids compression of any squeezed cell below this minimum action.

As $\theta$ varies, the squeezed cell rotates within the extended cell and changes aspect ratio. Its action stays at $h/2$. Its semi-axes track the support of the extended cell along $\theta$,
\begin{equation}
\delta_\parallel(\theta) = \sqrt{\Delta x^2\cos^2\theta + \Delta p^2\sin^2\theta},
\label{eq:delta_parallel}
\end{equation}
\begin{equation}
\delta_\perp(\theta) = \hbar / \delta_\parallel(\theta).
\label{eq:delta_perp}
\end{equation}
The angle $\theta$ is the rotated quadrature direction of continuous-variable tomography~\cite{vogelrisken1989,lvovsky2009}. At the principal-axis orientations $\theta = 0$ and $\theta = \pi/2$ (Fig.~\ref{fig:zurek_cells}), the short axes are Zurek's sub-Planck interference scales~\cite{zurek2001},
\begin{equation}
\delta x = \frac{\hbar}{\Delta p}, \qquad \delta p = \frac{\hbar}{\Delta x}.
\label{eq:zurek}
\end{equation}
The smallest cell a state can locally carry and the spacing of its interference fringes are the same length, set by the action of the state.

% ===============================================================================
%  THE DENSITY
% ===============================================================================

The Wigner function of the state,
\begin{equation}
W(x,p) = \frac{1}{\pi\hbar}\int \psi^*(x+y)\,\psi(x-y)\,e^{2ipy/\hbar}\,dy,
\label{eq:wigner}
\end{equation}
oscillates at the conjugate scale~\cite{wigner1932}. Sampling it at a finer scale would demand more action than the state carries. The limit $\delta_\parallel \to 0$ forces $\delta_\perp \to \infty$ by the conjugate identity, and the state cannot be located more finely than its own squeezed cell.

Looking at the state at this scale means weighting it by the squeezed coherent state of the same extent. The Wigner function of that squeezed coherent state at orientation $\theta$ is
\begin{equation}
K_\theta(x,p) = \frac{1}{\pi\hbar}\exp\!\left(-\frac{x_\theta^2}{\delta_\parallel^2} - \frac{P^2}{\delta_\perp^2}\right),
\label{eq:Gtheta}
\end{equation}
with $x_\theta = x\cos\theta + p\sin\theta$ and $P = -x\sin\theta + p\cos\theta$, of action $h/2$. The state seen at this resolution is
\begin{equation}
P_\theta(x,p) = (W * K_\theta)(x,p),
\label{eq:Ptheta}
\end{equation}
non-negative everywhere because the Wigner function of any state, weighted by the Wigner function of any state, is pointwise non-negative~\cite{degosson2005bullsci,degosson2013qblobs}. There are no free parameters. The weight carries action $h/2$ in any direction. The state's action sets the resolution at which it is everywhere positive, and that resolution is the reciprocal of the state's conjugate extent.

Methods from unrelated traditions reach the same effective resolution (Table~\ref{tab:effective_resolution}). At the parameter setting where each method works correctly, the resolved scale is $\delta x \sim \hbar/\Delta p$, the reciprocal of the conjugate extent of the state. At vacuum saturation the extent $\Delta x$ and the conjugate scale $\hbar/\Delta p$ coincide, and the reciprocal nature is invisible. As the state's action grows, the reciprocal opens, and resolution and extent move in opposite directions. The product of resolution and conjugate extent stays at $\hbar$, the action of one squeezed cell.

Two prior approaches reach a non-negative density from adjacent directions. Dragoman~\cite{dragoman2005} weights the Wigner function of the state by the Wigner function of a measuring apparatus, an external scale chosen by the experimenter. Cordero, de Gosson, D\"orfler, and Nicola~\cite{cordero2018} study Zurek's diamond cat state in the Born--Jordan portrait and find that the interference fringes are damped, a property of the portrait rather than of the state. The squeezed cell of action $h/2$ used here is intrinsic to the state itself, and Zurek's $\delta x$ and $\delta p$ are its principal-axis cases. Further comparison is in the Supplemental Material.

The Wigner function remains the phase-space portrait of the state. Its negative regions, the interference between alternative classical paths, are used as described at the start. When the state is read as a classical probability, the action it carries sets the resolution at which it is everywhere non-negative. Wigner resolution is the reciprocal of action.
