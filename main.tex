\title{Resolving Wigner Negativity and Semiclassical Caustics via Phase-Space Squeezing}

\begin{abstract}
We identify Planck's quantum of action not as a static constant, 
but as a dynamic symplectic structure: a conjugate pair of 
irreducible phase-space blobs whose geometry is squeezed by the 
system's classical action. This bounded resolution forbids the 
infinite kinematic action required to sustain semiclassical 
caustics, replacing divergent turning-point singularities with 
finite quantum densities.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}

Semiclassical mechanics maps classical orbits to oscillating quantum states. Yet it fails at physical boundaries, predicting infinite probability caustics at classical turning points. 

This failure stems from the assumption of infinite kinematic action. To achieve spatial localization at a turning point, the conjugate momentum must diverge to infinity. This extreme demands an infinite amount of action.

Physical systems do not possess infinite action. They are governed by the finite classical action $A$ and the invariant quantum of action $h$. Here, we treat $h$ not as a rigid phase-space cell, but as a dynamic symplectic geometry. By bounding the phase-space resolution to the finite action of the system, we forbid these semiclassical singularities, resolving the infinite caustics into finite probability amplitudes.

\section{The Symplectic Quantum of Action}

De Gosson \cite{degosson2009} translates the statistical Heisenberg limit, $\sigma_q \sigma_p \ge \hbar/2$, into classical semi-axes $\Delta q = \sqrt{2}\sigma_q$ and $\Delta p = \sqrt{2}\sigma_p$. This maps the statistical limit to the classical action area:
$$A = \pi \Delta q \Delta p \ge \frac{h}{2}.$$
With this equivalence established, we discard the statistical interpretation; hereafter, $\Delta q$ and $\Delta p$ serve as classical kinematic boundaries that define the area $h/2$.

The interior of this Hamiltonian boundary is structured by Zurek's reciprocal phase-space scales \cite{zurek2001}. Rooted in quantum metrology \cite{caves1981, meekhof1996}, spatial resolution is governed by the conjugate momentum boundary, $\delta q = \hbar / \Delta p$, and vice versa, $\delta p = \hbar / \Delta q$. 

Expressed as phase-space areas, these metrological bounds form a pair of centered, orthogonal ellipses:
$$a_q = \pi \delta q \Delta p = \frac{h}{2} \quad \text{and} \quad a_p = \pi \Delta q \delta p = \frac{h}{2}.$$
These constitute a conjugate pair of de Gosson's irreducible quantum blobs.

The symplectic structure of phase space demands that the quantum of 
action be carried by a conjugate pair. Position resolution and momentum 
resolution are symplectically dual---not independent. The quantum of 
action $h$ therefore admits a natural partition into $a_q$ and $a_p$, 
each irreducible and each carrying $h/2$. This is not a sum of two 
coincident areas; it is the unique decomposition of $h$ consistent 
with symplectic conjugacy.

We therefore identify Planck's constant as the sum of this conjugate pair:
$$h = a_q + a_p.$$
Geometrically, the quantum of action is a dynamic, symplectic structure (see Fig.~\ref{fig:quantum_of_action}).

In the normalized harmonic ground state, the blobs degenerate into a perfect circle where the classical action meets the quantum floor, $A = a_q = a_p = h/2$ (see Fig.~\ref{fig:qho_grid}, left column, top row). As $A$ increases, this symplectic exchange compresses the quantum blobs under the topological constraint of Gromov's non-squeezing theorem \cite{gromov1985}, shrinking $a_q$ and $a_p$, enforcing the correspondence principle.

While each blob upholds the invariant quantum floor ($h/2$), the squeezing via classical action $A$ collapses the orthogonal blobs $a_q$ and $a_p$ into fine crosshairs (see Fig.~\ref{fig:qho_grid}, left column, bottom row).

\section{Semiclassical Singularities}

While the semiclassical WKB approximation \cite{langer1937} translates classical trajectories into quantum amplitudes, its spatial probability density fails at the classical boundaries. The 1D projection relies on the classical momentum $p(q)$, modulated by a symplectic area:
\begin{equation}
P_{\text{WKB}}(q) \propto \frac{1}{p(q)} \cos^2\left(\frac{A_{\text{chord}}(q)}{\hbar} - \frac{\pi}{4}\right).
\end{equation}

At the turning points where classical momentum vanishes ($p \to 0$), the envelope exhibits a caustic divergence:
\begin{equation}
\lim_{q \to q_t} P_{\text{WKB}}(q) = \infty.
\end{equation}

Confined by these boundaries, the WKB approximation traps the projected spatial density between infinite caustics. This behavior represents a breakdown of the 1D semiclassical projection. Bridging these kinematic boundaries with finite observables requires a mechanism that accounts for finite action.

\section{Symplectic Resolution}

To resolve these singularities, we use the symplectic quantum of action, $h = a_q + a_p$, as a symplectic kernel $K_h$. We map the divergent 1D projection $P_{\text{WKB}}(q)$ to a symplectic density $P_h(q)$ by convolving it with the position support $\delta q$ of the quantum blob $a_q$.

Because the spatial support $\delta q$ is bounded by the reciprocal momentum, $\delta q = \hbar / \Delta p$, the symplectic kernel $K_h$ must saturate this invariant area without violating Gromov's non-squeezing theorem. In de Gosson's geometric framework, the Gaussian is not a statistical distribution, but the canonical irreducible profile that saturates the symplectic capacity $h/2$ \cite{degosson2009}. Therefore, we define $K_h$ as the normalized Gaussian profile of this geometric limit. Substituting for $\delta q$ reveals the classical momentum, $\Delta p$, regulating the position boundary:
$$K_h(q) = \frac{2\sqrt{\pi} \Delta p}{h} \exp\left( - \left( \frac{2\pi \Delta p \, q}{h} \right)^2 \right).$$

The symplectic density $P_h(q)$ is then obtained through the convolution of the semiclassical density $P_{\text{WKB}}(q)$ with this kernel:
$$P_h(q) = (P_{\text{WKB}} * K_h)(q) = \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} P_{\text{WKB}}(q') K_h(q - q') \, dq'.$$

Whereas the Husimi distribution \cite{husimi1940} coarse-grains by the vacuum---a 
coherent-state overlap imposed identically on every state---the 
symplectic kernel $K_h$ is not chosen but forced: it is squeezed 
by the classical action $A$ of the system itself. Because the 
Husimi kernel is state-independent, it is structurally incapable 
of enforcing the correspondence principle. The symplectc kernel $K_h$ tightens as $A$ 
grows, its evanescent tails shrinking toward the classical trajectory.

Although the semiclassical density diverges at the turning points, the singularity scales as $1/\sqrt{q}$, meaning the localized probability remains finite. Sustaining an infinite spatial density requires the conjugate momentum boundary to diverge to infinity---an extreme that demands infinite action.

Because the available action is limited by the symplectic quantum of action $h$, this unbounded divergence is forbidden. Integrating the semiclassical singularities against the symplectic kernel $K_h$ distributes the finite probability across the $\delta q$ width of the quantum blob. The presence of $h$ prevents this extreme, transforming the non-physical caustics into finite, observable probability amplitudes.

The symplectic resolution treats nodes and caustics symmetrically: 
both require infinite kinematic action to sustain. A caustic demands 
$\Delta p \to \infty$ to localize probability at a point; a node 
demands the same infinite phase-space resolution to collapse it to 
zero. The irreducible quantum blob $a_q = h/2$ forbids both extremes 
--- the finite geometry of classical action can neither concentrate 
probability into a spike nor extinguish it at a point.
    
\section{The Harmonic Oscillator}

We demonstrate the symplectic resolution of semiclassical singularities via the harmonic oscillator (see Fig.~\ref{fig:qho_grid}). Following Berry's semiclassical formulation \cite{berry1977}, the 2D Wigner phase-space distribution \cite{wigner1932} oscillates according to the area $A_{\text{chord}}(q,p)$ bounded between the classical trajectory and a chord bisected by $(q,p)$:
$$W(q,p) \propto \cos\left( \frac{A_{\text{chord}}(q,p)}{\hbar} - \frac{\pi}{4} \right).$$

Standard projections of this 2D distribution onto the 1D spatial axis treat the classical boundary as an infinitely sharp curve, inevitably spawning unphysical caustics. 

Rather than patching these turning points with Airy functions \cite{langer1937}, we use our 2D symplectic projection. Integrating the density against the symplectic kernel $K_h$ enforces the irreducible phase-space area $a_q = h/2$. This bounded spatial resolution physically truncates the caustic divergence, seamlessly transforming the classical boundaries into the finite probability amplitudes characteristic of discrete quantum states.

\section{Discussion}

Semiclassical caustics are not physical. They are artifacts of assuming infinite kinematic action. 

When action is finite, these singularities vanish. Classical action $A=\pi \Delta q \Delta p$ squeezes the conjugate coordinate resolutions $\delta q = \hbar / \Delta p$ and $\delta p = \hbar / \Delta q$, but the invariant quantum of action, $h$, forbids a collapse to zero. 

The divergent caustics represent a mathematical extrapolation to infinity. By instead treating classical action as a finite, physical resource that scales the quantum-to-classical transition, these semiclassical singularities are forbidden, naturally resolving into finite quantum densities.
