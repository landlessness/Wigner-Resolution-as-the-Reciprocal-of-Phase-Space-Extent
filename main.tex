\title{Resolving Wigner Negativity and Semiclassical Caustics via Finite Classical Action}

\begin{abstract}
Wigner negativity and semiclassical caustics both follow from assuming unbounded classical action. Enforcing the finite action of a physical system via symplectic phase-space cells resolves both simultaneously. The resulting densities are provably non-negative and free of turning-point divergences.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}

Phase-space representations of quantum mechanics bridge classical trajectories and quantum amplitudes, but the mapping introduces two persistent pathologies. The Wigner--Weyl distribution exhibits negative values wherever quantum interference is strong \cite{wigner1932}, while the WKB probability density diverges at classical turning points where the local momentum vanishes \cite{berry1977,langer1937}. These are typically treated as separate inconveniences of separate formalisms.

They are not. Both pathologies are kinematic consequences of the same assumption: that classical action is unbounded. Wigner negativity arises from interference on scales below $\hbar/\Delta p$; the WKB caustic arises from demanding $\delta q \to 0$ at the turning point, which forces $\Delta p \to \infty$ and hence infinite classical action. No physical system satisfies this condition.

The standard remedy applies a fixed Gaussian kernel to yield the Husimi Q-function \cite{husimi1940}, which suppresses negativity and softens the caustics. But the Husimi kernel is set once by the ground state and carries no knowledge of the system's classical action --- a kernel ignorant of $A$ cannot resolve structure governed by $A$, and introduces artifactual tails broader than true quantum tunneling.

Here we enforce what the physics already demands: a finite classical action $A$ constrains the phase-space resolution to Zurek's reciprocal scales $\delta q = \hbar/\Delta p$ and $\delta p = \hbar/\Delta q$ \cite{zurek2001}. Drawing on de~Gosson's symplectic geometry \cite{degosson2009,degosson2012fermi} and Gromov's non-squeezing theorem \cite{gromov1985}, we construct squeezed Gaussian kernels governed entirely by $A$. These kernels saturate the Heisenberg limit, provably eliminate Wigner negativity by Hudson's theorem \cite{hudson1974}, and bound the momentum support at turning points, replacing WKB divergences with finite-amplitude densities. Finite classical action resolves Wigner negativity and semiclassical caustics.

\section{Wigner Negativity and Semiclassical Caustics}
The Wigner--Weyl distribution furnishes an exact phase-space representation of a quantum state $\psi$,
$$W(q,p) = \frac{1}{\pi\hbar}\int_{-\infty}^{\infty} \psi^*(q+x)\psi(q-x)\, e^{2ipx/\hbar}\, dx,$$
but allows negative values wherever quantum interference is strong \cite{wigner1932}. Negativity is not a numerical artifact; it is a structural feature of the Wigner function for any pure state beyond the ground state, and no phase-space rotation or scaling removes it.

The semiclassical limit approaches the problem from the opposite direction. The WKB probability density,
$$P_\text{WKB}(q) \propto \frac{1}{\sqrt{E - V(q)}},$$
diverges at the classical turning points $q_\pm$ where $E = V(q)$ and the local momentum vanishes \cite{berry1977, langer1937}. Physically, the particle slows to rest at the boundary; statistically, it spends infinite time there. The divergence is not regularized by any finite order of the WKB expansion.

Both pathologies share a kinematic root. Wigner negativity arises from interference on scales below $\hbar/\Delta p$, and the WKB caustic arises from assuming $\delta q \to 0$ at the turning point — which demands $\Delta p \to \infty$ and hence infinite classical action $A$. A representation bounded by finite $A$ cannot support either pathology.

\section{Phase-Space Squeezing}

De Gosson maps the Heisenberg limit $\sigma_q \sigma_p \ge \hbar/2$ into semi-axes $\Delta q = \sqrt{2}\sigma_q$, $\Delta p = \sqrt{2}\sigma_p$, projecting the $1\sigma$ contour onto a Fermi blob \cite{degosson2012fermi} with phase-space area
$$A = \pi \Delta q \Delta p \ge \frac{h}{2}.$$
Hereafter, $\Delta q$ and $\Delta p$ are classical kinematic boundaries.

Zurek's reciprocal scales \cite{zurek2001}, rooted in quantum metrology \cite{caves1981,meekhof1996}, partition the blob interior: $\delta q = \hbar/\Delta p$ and $\delta p = \hbar/\Delta q$. These define a conjugate pair of orthogonal quantum blobs \cite{degosson2009},
$$a_q = \pi\,\delta q\,\Delta p = \frac{h}{2}, \qquad a_p = \pi\,\Delta q\,\delta p = \frac{h}{2},$$
each carrying the irreducible area $h/2$.

De Gosson shows that $A$ must contain both quantum blobs $a_q$ and $a_p$. Holding their areas fixed at $h/2$ while the macroscopic boundaries $\Delta p$ and $\Delta q$ expand forces reciprocal compression of $\delta q$ and $\delta p$---a topological necessity enforced by Gromov's non-squeezing theorem \cite{gromov1985} (Fig.~\ref{fig:quantum_of_action}).

In the normalized harmonic ground state the three blobs coincide: $A = a_q = a_p = h/2$, a perfect circle at the quantum floor (Fig.~\ref{fig:qho_grid}, top-left). As $A$ grows, expanding $\Delta q$, $\Delta p$ stretch $a_q$ and $a_p$ into fine crosshairs (Fig.~\ref{fig:qho_grid}, bottom-left), recovering the precision of the correspondence principle.

\section{Resolution via Phase-Space Squeezing}

The classical action $A = \pi \Delta q \Delta p$ sets the phase-space resolution via the reciprocal scales $\delta q = \hbar/\Delta p$ and $\delta p = \hbar/\Delta q$. Following de Gosson \cite{degosson2009, degosson2012fermi}, the minimum-uncertainty blobs $a_q$ and $a_p$ of area $h/2$ consistent with these bounds take the Gaussian form of the conjugate squeezed kernels,
$$G_{\delta q}(q,p) = \frac{1}{\pi\hbar}\exp\!\left(-\frac{q^2}{\delta q^2} - \frac{p^2}{\Delta p^2}\right),$$
$$G_{\delta p}(q,p) = \frac{1}{\pi\hbar}\exp\!\left(-\frac{q^2}{\Delta q^2} - \frac{p^2}{\delta p^2}\right),$$
where $G_{\delta q}$ is squeezed in $q$ and spans $\Delta p$, and $G_{\delta p}$ is squeezed in $p$ and spans $\Delta q$. Each kernel carries symplectic area $h/2$, saturating the Heisenberg limit. The conjugate squeezed densities are then
$$P_{\delta q} = W * G_{\delta q}, \qquad P_{\delta p} = W * G_{\delta p},$$
with 1D projections
$$P_{\delta q}(q) = \int P_{\delta q}(q,p)\, dp, \qquad P_{\delta p}(p) = \int P_{\delta p}(q,p)\, dq.$$
Convolving $W$ with a Gaussian kernel of area $h/2$ yields a non-negative distribution \cite{degosson2009}; the finite momentum support $\delta p = \hbar/\Delta q$ simultaneously bounds the kinematic action at the classical turning points, replacing WKB caustics with finite-amplitude densities.

The squeezed densities $P_{\delta q}$ and $P_{\delta p}$ differ fundamentally from the Husimi Q-function \cite{husimi1940}. Husimi convolves $W$ with a fixed coherent-state kernel whose scale is set once by the ground state, with no knowledge of the system's classical action. The squeezed kernels $G_{\delta q}$ and $G_{\delta p}$, by contrast, are governed by $A$: a physical system with finite classical action cannot support phase-space resolution finer than $\delta q$ or $\delta p$, so the kernels are not a choice but a consequence. Where the isotropic Husimi blur is blind to $A$ and smears indiscriminately, $G_{\delta q}$ and $G_{\delta p}$ sharpen anisotropically along the classical trajectory, recovering features the fixed kernel permanently obscures.
