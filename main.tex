\title{Quantum of Action as Two Conjugate Cells Resolves Caustics and Wigner Negativity}

\begin{abstract}
A quantum state has finite action in phase space. Heisenberg's uncertainty combined with de Gosson's topology forces this action into two conjugate cells of size $h/2$, one squeezed along position, the other along momentum. Caustics in the WKB density and negativity in the Wigner function are mathematical artifacts because both formalisms calculate below this scale. At the physical scale, the state is everywhere finite and non-negative.
\end{abstract}

% ======================================================================
% SLICE 1 --- PHYSICS
% ======================================================================

A quantum state has finite action in phase space. The Heisenberg and Robertson--Schr\"odinger uncertainty principles \cite{robertson1929,schrodinger1930},
\begin{equation}
\sigma_q \sigma_p \ge \hbar/2,
\label{eq:hup}
\end{equation}
forbid the state from being a point: zero area is incompatible with finite uncertainty.

\begin{figure}[t]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=\linewidth]{figures/quantum_of_action.pdf}
\caption{\textbf{Conjugate structure of the quantum state} (schematic, 1D case). The Heisenberg limit $\sigma_q \sigma_p \ge \hbar/2$ admits semi-axes $\Delta q = \sqrt{2}\sigma_q$, $\Delta p = \sqrt{2}\sigma_p$; the state's $1\sigma$ contour is its Fermi blob \cite{degosson2013fermiblobs}, of area $A = \pi\Delta q\Delta p \ge h/2$. Inside sit two conjugate quantum blobs \cite{degosson2009,degossoncharlyne2022}, $a_q = \pi\,\delta q\,\Delta p = h/2$ squeezed along position and $a_p = \pi\,\Delta q\,\delta p = h/2$ squeezed along momentum, summing to $h = a_q + a_p$. Their narrow widths are $\delta q = \hbar/\Delta p$ and $\delta p = \hbar/\Delta q$. As $A$ grows, $\Delta p$ and $\Delta q$ grow while $\delta q$ and $\delta p$ shrink: Heisenberg holds at every scale shown.}
\label{fig:quantum_of_action}
\end{figure}

De Gosson identified the state's extent in phase space as the Fermi blob \cite{degosson2013fermiblobs}, the elliptical region defined by its covariance with semi-axes $\Delta q = \sqrt{2}\sigma_q$ and $\Delta p = \sqrt{2}\sigma_p$. Its covariance area,
\begin{equation}
A = \pi \Delta q \Delta p \ge h/2,
\label{eq:fermi_blob_area}
\end{equation}
is distinct from the system's dynamical action, saturating at $h/2$ only for the exact harmonic ground state. A Fermi blob of covariance area $A > h/2$ is not featureless. Its interior holds two conjugate quantum blobs \cite{degosson2009,degossoncharlyne2022},
\begin{equation}
a_q = \pi\,\delta q\,\Delta p = h/2, \qquad a_p = \pi\,\Delta q\,\delta p = h/2,
\label{eq:quantum_blobs}
\end{equation}
each carrying half the Planck quantum of action and together summing to $h$ (Fig.~\ref{fig:quantum_of_action}). The narrow widths
\begin{equation}
\delta q = \hbar/\Delta p, \qquad \delta p = \hbar/\Delta q
\label{eq:reciprocal}
\end{equation}
are reciprocal to the Fermi-blob semi-axes, fixed by the same Heisenberg constant in Eq.~(\ref{eq:hup}). Equation~(\ref{eq:reciprocal}) is itself an uncertainty relation between conjugate resolutions inside a state of fixed covariance. As $A$ grows, $\Delta p$ and $\Delta q$ grow while $\delta q$ and $\delta p$ shrink: Heisenberg binds the state at both scales. Gromov's non-squeezing theorem \cite{gromov1985} forbids any canonical transformation from compressing a quantum blob below $h/2$. Planck's quantum of action is the floor of phase space.

% ======================================================================
% SLICE 2 --- FORMALISM PROBLEMS
% ======================================================================

Two pathologies arise when formalisms breach this floor. The WKB density \cite{berry1977}
\begin{equation}
|\psi_\text{WKB}(q)|^2 = \frac{N^2}{p(q)}\cos^2\!\left(S(q)/\hbar - \pi/4\right)
\label{eq:wkb}
\end{equation}
diverges at every classical turning point. The Wigner--Weyl distribution \cite{wigner1932}
\begin{equation}
W(q,p) = \frac{1}{\pi\hbar}\int_{-\infty}^{\infty} \psi^*(q+y)\,\psi(q-y)\,e^{2ipy/\hbar}\, dy
\label{eq:wigner}
\end{equation}
takes negative values for any state beyond the ground state. Connection formulas and Airy uniformization \cite{langer1937} patch the caustic locally; the Husimi $Q$-function \cite{husimi1940} smooths Wigner with a fixed coherent-state kernel of unit width and erases every scale below it. Each remedy treats one symptom in one formalism.

The two pathologies share a single root. WKB evaluates density at a precise $q$; Wigner evaluates it at an exact $(q,p)$. Each assumes a zero-area point evaluation ($\delta q \to 0$ or $\delta p \to 0$), which is physically forbidden \cite{dragoman2005}. By Eq.~(\ref{eq:reciprocal}) this demands an infinite conjugate spread ($\Delta p \to \infty$ or $\Delta q \to \infty$) and unbounded covariance area ($A \to \infty$). Caustics and Wigner negativity are mathematical artifacts of this forbidden limit, not features of the state.

% ======================================================================
% SLICE 3 --- PHYSICS
% ======================================================================

The two conjugate quantum blobs are the physical scale of the state. Because pointwise evaluation ignores this floor, we adapt the phase-space filtering of \cite{dragoman2005} with a kernel set by the state's own quantum blobs. The conjugate quantum blobs of Fig.~\ref{fig:quantum_of_action}, as Gaussian filters of phase-space area $h/2$, give
\begin{equation}
G_{\delta q}(q,p) = \frac{1}{\pi}\exp\!\left(-\frac{q^2}{\delta q^2} - \frac{p^2}{\Delta p^2}\right),
\label{eq:Gdq}
\end{equation}
\begin{equation}
G_{\delta p}(q,p) = \frac{1}{\pi}\exp\!\left(-\frac{q^2}{\Delta q^2} - \frac{p^2}{\delta p^2}\right),
\label{eq:Gdp}
\end{equation}
$G_{\delta q}$ squeezed along position and $G_{\delta p}$ along momentum. Each kernel is itself a quantum blob at the $h/2$ floor. Unlike Husimi's fixed-width kernel, $G_{\delta q}$ tracks the state's own covariance.

The classical orbit at the Bohr--Sommerfeld energy $E_n$ provides kernel widths $\Delta q = \sqrt{2\langle (q-\langle q\rangle)^2\rangle_\text{orbit}}$ and $\Delta p = \sqrt{2\langle p^2\rangle_\text{orbit}}$. We resolve the oscillating WKB phase-space density $W_\text{WKB}(q,p) \propto \delta(H - E)\,\cos^2(S(q)/\hbar - \pi/4)$, whose $p$-marginal is the diverging $|\psi_\text{WKB}|^2$. The convolved marginal,
\begin{equation}
\rho_{\delta q}(q) = \int (W_\text{WKB} * G_{\delta q})(q,p)\, dp,
\label{eq:rho_marginal}
\end{equation}
is finite at the turning points and strictly positive everywhere due to the kernel's Gaussian tails. Figure~\ref{fig:semiclassical} shows three systems. The harmonic ground state collapses to a single Gaussian. The Morse oscillator at $n=8$ preserves the $n+1$ Bohr--Sommerfeld interference lobes; the WKB caustics become finite peaks. For the symmetric double-well sub-barrier doublet, per-well Langer--Airy constructions place artificial maxima at the inner turning points; $\rho_{\delta q}(q)$ is everywhere finite and seamlessly resolves the physical barrier penetration.

The same kernel applies in the wave formalism, with $\Delta q = \sqrt{2\langle (q-\langle q\rangle)^2\rangle_{\psi_n}}$ and $\Delta p = \sqrt{2\langle p^2\rangle_{\psi_n}}$ from the eigenstate's Robertson--Schr\"odinger covariance. The convolved Wigner density
\begin{equation}
P_{\delta q}(q,p) = (W * G_{\delta q})(q,p)
\label{eq:Pdq}
\end{equation}
is non-negative everywhere: convolution with a quantum-blob kernel sends any Wigner function to a non-negative one \cite{hudson1974,degosson2013qblobs}. Figure~\ref{fig:wigner} shows the three systems as eigenstates. The Morse row at $n=8$ has $n+1$ alternating-sign Wigner lobes; $P_{\delta q}$ preserves the lobe envelope and is non-negative throughout, while Husimi smooths away the interior interference, leaving only two broad peaks at the classical turning points. The double-well antisymmetric partner $\psi_1$ has an exact wavefunction node at $q=0$, which coincides with $W_1(0,0) = -0.32$, the deepest point of the Wigner negativity --- the same unphysical point-evaluation failing in two different formalisms. At resolution $\delta q$, $P_{\delta q}(q,0)$ is strictly positive throughout, including across the node and through the barrier where the Gaussian tails extend from each well.

A cat state has no classical orbit and no Bohr--Sommerfeld action. The Fermi blob is still finite, set by the wavefunction's covariance directly, with $\Delta q = \sqrt{2\langle (q-\langle q\rangle)^2\rangle_\psi}$ and $\Delta p = \sqrt{2\langle p^2\rangle_\psi}$. Figure~\ref{fig:cats} shows four cat configurations. Each row's Wigner cross-section dips below zero --- the central interference fringe of the 2-cat, the pair-fringe envelopes of the 3-cat, and the sub-Planck chessboards of the two 4-cat compasses. Zurek's sub-Planck chessboard \cite{zurek2001} is the conjugate quantum-blob width $\delta q = \hbar/\Delta p$. At resolution $\delta q$, every cross-section is non-negative.

% ======================================================================
% SLICE 4 --- CLOSING
% ======================================================================

A quantum state has finite action in phase space and cannot be reduced to a point. Caustics and Wigner negativity are mathematical artifacts of ignoring this physical reality. That action is partitioned by Heisenberg's uncertainty and de Gosson's topology into a Fermi blob and two conjugate quantum blobs. At the physical scale, the state is everywhere finite and non-negative.
